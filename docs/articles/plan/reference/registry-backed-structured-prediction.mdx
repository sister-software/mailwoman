# Registry-backed structured prediction

How Mailwoman is allowed to use address registries — BAN, BAG, TIGER, WOF, OSM — and
why they feed the model instead of deciding for it.

The one-sentence version:

> **Registries provide soft lexical priors and synthetic coverage. The model still owns
> ambiguity.**

This is the street-level generalization of
[closed-vocab fields: model-first](./closed-vocab-fields-model-first.mdx). That doc
settled `country` / `po_box` / `cedex`: the gazetteer enters as a soft feature, never a
substitution. This one settles street names, where the pull toward substitution is much
stronger — because a national register does know every street, and it is very tempting to
let it say so.

## What this is not

Two architectures sit on either side of this design, and both have already failed here.
Naming them is the point of the doc: every future convenience will look like a small step
toward one of them.

### Not a dictionary parser

Pelias matches phrases against curated dictionaries. It works extraordinarily well on
exact template matches and fails hard on messy input, because the hypothesis space is
**closed** — a phrase the dictionary doesn't carry cannot be proposed at all. The
receipts are in the dictionaries themselves: read
`pelias/parser/resources/pelias/dictionaries` and every file is a story about a synonym
or word fragment that broke something. The line between scar tissue and pragmatism is
ambiguous in there, and that ambiguity is the cost of the architecture, not a lapse in
its maintenance.

A register used as a veto is a dictionary parser wearing a probability. It fails on the
same input for the same reason: an unseen street is punished for being unseen.

### Not resolver-as-arbiter

Phase 4a tried to let the resolver adjudicate between k-best readings. It doesn't work,
and the reason is structural rather than a tuning miss: **the resolver needs a house
number or a locality to resolve, so it is blind exactly where bare fragments need it.**
`Rue Montmartre` alone gives it nothing to resolve, so it cannot arbitrate the one class
that motivated the arc. This was measured, not assumed — see the Phase 4a verdict.

Both failures share a shape. Something downstream of the model was asked to supply
certainty the model didn't have, and it supplied confidence instead.

## The two channels

A registry gets exactly two ways in. Everything else stays with the model.

### 1. Soft lexical prior

Positive-only bias, weighted by source tier and scope, with the constant tuned **per
locale family** rather than once globally.

### 2. Synthetic coverage

Phenomenon shards at a capped ratio, split source-disjoint by normalized street surface.
If the model has never seen a bare street, no amount of inference-time evidence fixes the
prior — that's a training-distribution problem and it gets a training-distribution fix.

## The provider

One abstraction, so a tier can never quietly acquire a capability it wasn't granted:

```ts
interface StreetNameEvidenceProvider {
	lookup(surface: string, locale: LocaleHint, scope?: EvidenceScope): StreetNameEvidence
}

interface StreetNameEvidence {
	exists: boolean
	confidence: number
	source: string
	sourceTier: EvidenceTier // A | B | C | D
	scopeLevel: ScopeLevel // global | region | locality | postcode
	frequency: number
}
```

`sourceTier` is part of the return, not something a consumer derives by pattern-matching
`source`. Confidence depends on tier and scope, so tier has to be legible at the point
the bias is applied.

### Rules

1. **Positive evidence only.**
2. **Confidence depends on source tier and scope.**
3. **No global vetoes.**
4. **Tune bias per locale family**, not one universal constant.
5. **Split eval/train by street surface**, not record row.

Rule 1 is the one that will be under pressure. Negative evidence from an incomplete
register recreates the Pelias deletion in soft form: real, unseen streets punished
globally, and the punishment is invisible because it looks like a probability instead of
a missing dictionary entry. No tier — including the complete-in-principle ones — supplies
global negative evidence.

Rule 5 has already caught us once in spirit: a row-disjoint split leaks the surface
across the boundary, and you measure memorization of `Rue de Rivoli` while believing you
measured generalization to unseen streets.

## Capability tiers

| Tier  | Data                                                         | Allowed use                                  | Not allowed                                                         |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **A** | Authoritative street/address registers — FR BAN, NL BAG-like | Eval, augmentation, positive name-index bias | Negative evidence                                                   |
| **B** | Road/address-range data — US TIGER                           | Positive bias **only with scope**            | Unscoped/global evidence; negative evidence                         |
| **C** | Admin gazetteers — WOF                                       | Locality/region/country evidence             | **Street existence**; negative evidence                             |
| **D** | OSM-like broad data                                          | Experiments, coverage-gap analysis           | Product use without licensing/attribution review; negative evidence |

### Tier A — start here

BAN is the first proving ground because it is clean, national, and street-name complete.
It's the only tier where absence is even arguably informative, and the only place the
augmentation can be graded against something that isn't a guess. Generalization to other
locales happens **by tier, afterward** — never folded into the same step.

### Tier B — scope is the whole rule

`Main St` globally is weak evidence; it exists in nearly every US locality, so its
existence discriminates nothing. `Main St` + `CA` / `Santa Clara` / a ZIP is useful.

There's a trap here worth stating plainly, because it's the same one that killed Phase
4a: **scoping re-introduces the locality dependency.** If the scope has to come from a
resolve, Tier B is blind on exactly the bare fragments that need it. Tier B evidence is
usable only when the scope is present in the input.

### Tier C — the famous-street-as-place trap

WOF carries admin polygons. It also carries famous streets **as places** — and `rue`,
`boulevard`, and `place` are themselves gazetteer entries. Reading a WOF hit as street
existence inverts it: `Rue Montmartre` looks like evidence for `locality`, which is the
exact error the arc is trying to fix.

This is measured, not asserted. Walking the shipped FST (`fst-global-priority.bin`) over
the Paris fixtures returns 43 distinct surfaces, 37 of which would fire an emission bias:

| surface        | placetype : importance                |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| `rue`          | localadmin 0.145, neighbourhood 0.149 |
| `boulevard`    | neighbourhood 0.167                   |
| `place`        | neighbourhood 0.169                   |
| `la`           | locality **0.850**                    |
| `du`           | region **0.650**                      |
| `londres`      | region 0.937, locality 0.936          |
| `saintgermain` | locality 0.386                        |
| `12`, `2`, …   | localadmin 0.05–0.15                  |

The French particles carry the strongest hits on the board — `la` at 0.850 locality,
inside street phrases like `Rue de la Paix` — and bare house numbers match localadmin
places. So the trap's _ingredients_ are unambiguously present.

**What is not present is the effect.** Turning the prior on moves the Paris target class
by nothing: 33/63 off, 33/63 on, two fixtures changing in opposite directions. So the
feedback loop is not what fails these addresses today — the model's locality reading is
its own, learned from a corpus where bare street fragments are rare and bare localities
are not. That is a training-distribution problem, which is why the BAN shard outranks
every inference-time lever.

Keep the tier rule anyway. It does not rest on a measured harm — it rests on the
encoding. A channel that answers "is this a locality" must never be read as answering "is
this a street," and the table above is exactly what a future consumer would be reading if
it did.

Tier C answers locality/region/country. It does not answer "is this a street."

### Tier D — licensing gates product use

OSM's coverage is the best available for gap analysis and for experiments that need
breadth. Source quality, licensing, and attribution gate anything that ships. Note
`@mailwoman/osm` is itself unpublished pending ODbL counsel sign-off.

## Training: phenomenon shards

Per locale, sampled from the Tier A register:

1. **bare street** — `Rue Montmartre`
2. **street + particle** — `Rue de la Paix`, `Avenue des Champs-Élysées`
3. **street + house number**
4. **admin/street homonym** — the polarity class
5. **date-name** — `Rue du 11-Novembre-1918`
6. **alphanumeric house number**

**Shard ratios stay capped** so full-address performance doesn't regress. The cap is the
mechanism; the contextful regression guard is the check that the mechanism worked. A
shard that fixes fragments by degrading normal addresses hasn't fixed anything — it moved
the failure somewhere the fragment board can't see.

Label policy for street shards: the **full street phrase** is `street`, including affix,
particle, apostrophe, hyphenated compound, and date-name material.

## Eval: two boards, three questions

| Board                     | Question                    | Instrument                                                    |
| ------------------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Global parity floor**   | Do no harm                  | `mailwoman eval parity` — broad, the standing floors          |
| **Locale fragment board** | Did the targeted class move | `mailwoman eval fragment-board` — per-class, Wilson intervals |

A change ships when board 1 holds **and** board 2 moves. Neither board alone is a verdict.

Why two, concretely: the span-head arc scored +23.8pp on its target class and roughly +0
net overall. A single blended number turns that into "inside noise, ship it" — and hides
both the win and whatever the win was paid for. A 3/15 cell on a 63-row fixture has a 95%
Wilson interval of 7–45%, which is not a measurement. The fragment board samples BAN at
~400/class so the targeted classes get error bars instead of anecdotes.

Its first read (v264, the shipped model) is why the board earns its keep — same streets,
same model, the only difference a leading number:

| class                             |   n |  rate | 95% CI         |
| --------------------------------- | --: | ----: | -------------- |
| street-housenumber                | 400 | 0.925 | [0.895, 0.947] |
| alnum-housenumber                 | 400 | 0.925 | [0.895, 0.947] |
| bare-street                       | 400 | 0.215 | [0.178, 0.258] |
| street-particle                   | 400 | 0.273 | [0.231, 0.318] |
| admin-street-homonym              | 400 | 0.087 | [0.064, 0.119] |
| date-name                         | 400 | 0.055 | [0.037, 0.082] |
| bare-locality _(emits no street)_ | 400 | 0.980 | [0.961, 0.990] |

**The house number is the entire signal.** With one, 92.5%; without one, 21.5%. The
intervals do not come close to touching, so this is not a reading anyone has to squint at.

### The third question: what does the board _not_ score?

Two boards were not enough, and the arc proved it. Every street metric in the repo filters
to rows carrying a street gold, so a street **hallucinated on an input that has none** was
invisible to all of them — `New York, NY` → `street="new york"` could never cost a point.
The span decode's failure lived precisely in the rows the filter dropped, which means the
instrument flattered that decode **specifically**.

So the fragment board carries a **negative class**: `bare-locality` rows asserting the
parser emits _no_ street. A board that cannot score the failure cannot grade the fix.

Generalize the habit, because this was the third instrument failure in one arc (starved
channels, a dark resolver, a filtered corpus): before trusting a board, ask what it drops.
A registered baseline will not save you — a baseline over a filtered corpus is
self-consistent forever. It pins reproducibility, not validity.

## Why this holds

The failure the arc is fixing is three interacting things: a training distribution that
underrepresents bare streets, an objective that rewards token correctness over span
utility, and resolver evidence that is blind where arbitration is needed. Registries
address the first. They are not permitted to paper over the second or the third, because
that's how a parser becomes a dictionary — one convenience at a time, each individually
defensible, until the hypothesis space is closed again and the messy addresses fail.

The model owns ambiguity. That's the whole line.
