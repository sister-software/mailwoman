# Which languages Mailwoman handles well

If you've evaluated geocoders before, you've seen the coverage page: a world map,
mostly colored in, with the caveats in a footnote. We'd rather tell you where the
footnotes live. Mailwoman only claims a locale when a coordinate-graded eval
exists for it — the internal ledger is the tier table in the
[scope declaration](../plan/SCOPE.mdx), and this page is the plain-language read of
that table.

"Handles well" is three separate claims, and they don't move together.

1. **Parse quality** — does the model [label the parts of an address](./how-mailwoman-parses-an-address.mdx) correctly?
2. **Coordinate resolution** — does the resolved point land in the right place?
3. **Rooftop data** — is there an open address register behind it, so a house
   number resolves to the building instead of the street or the town?

A language can score well on one layer and poorly on another. The tiers below say
which layers back each claim.

## First class: US English and French

Both carry per-tag floors in the release gate — a model that regresses either does
not ship. Both have rooftop-grade open registers wired in: the US situs layer
(124.9M address points, built from state and federal open data) and the French BAN
(26M points, Licence Ouverte). A house number in Lyon or Louisville resolves to the
building, at 1 m reported uncertainty, and both paths are pinned by regression
suites that run in the browser against production.

French is the more instructive story. The tokenizer originally fragmented accented
street names ("Allée" split at the "é" and dropped the word), which cost
resolution on exactly the addresses a French user types first. Fixing it took a
vocabulary splice plus a fine-tune so the merged pieces got gradient steps — the
training-free shortcut measurably made things worse before the retrain fixed it.
The lesson generalizes: diacritics are a tokenizer problem before they are a model
problem, and we now check fertility on every new locale.

## Coordinate-paneled: most of western and central Europe, plus Australia

Italian, Portuguese, Polish, Czech, German, Spanish, Dutch, Croatian, Slovak,
Danish, and Finnish are trained locales with per-locale coordinate panels (n=1000
each) behind them, resolving with p90 under 10 km everywhere and much better in
most places. Dutch is the standout: block-level postcodes take it to 0.05 km
median. Austrian, Belgian, and Swiss addresses ride the German/French/Dutch
handling with their own panel rows. Australian English trained on G-NAF, the
national address register, but that register only fed the training corpus — no
AU rooftop shard is wired into the resolver, so Australian addresses resolve at
the same locality-and-postcode grade as the rest of this tier.

German also carries a gate floor of its own, and the West Slavic set (Czech,
Polish, Slovak, Slovenian) got the same tokenizer-splice treatment as French —
their wrong-city rates sit in single digits.

One caveat for this tier: resolution is locality-and-postcode grade, not rooftop.
A Warsaw address lands in the right neighborhood, not on the roof, because no open
national register is wired in yet.

## Thin: Norwegian, Swedish, Slovenian

Each of these is trained and paneled, and each has a named failure we track. Norway
resolves at 92.7% with a long residual tail. The Swedish exonym class (Åbo for
Turku) and the Slovenian leading-name boundary class (village names that start
like street names) are improvement-target rows in the regression gauntlet: they run
on every release, and they don't pass yet. If your
data is heavy in these locales, try it on your sample before committing.

## Structured differently: Japanese

Japan resolves by postcode route through a per-country convention engine, not
through the neural parser — Japanese addressing is regular enough that rules beat
a sequence labeler there, and we make no parse-quality claim. It works well for
what it is (~95% on the convention suite); it is a different mechanism with
different failure modes. Taiwan and Korea are open work, not claims.

## Known gap: British English

The one evaluators poke at first, so here it is up front. UK addresses parse
better than you'd expect — a postcode-format prior routes them correctly most of
the time — but there is no UK training corpus, because the usable open data
carries a share-alike license that conflicts with how we distribute training
shards. The blocker is licensing, not modeling, and the issue tracking it has a
check-back owner. Ireland, Hungary, and Romania sit behind the same gate.

Non-Latin scripts (Cyrillic, Hangul, Han, Armenian) have synthetic transliteration
exposure in the corpus, so the parser degrades gracefully instead of shattering.
We don't count graceful degradation as support.

## Short version

Floor-gated on US English and French with rooftop data behind both;
coordinate-paneled across ~14 European locales at sub-10 km p90; Japan by
postcode route; Britain is the known gap, blocked on licensing. When a locale is
missing from this page, that is also a claim: we haven't measured it, so we don't
say it works.
