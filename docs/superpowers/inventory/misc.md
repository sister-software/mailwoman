## Section notes

- **The headline finding is status.mdx.** It calls itself "the single source of truth for what works in Mailwoman right now," carries the highest inbound-link count of any page in this scope (8), and is stuck on a "Verified as of 2026-07-02 — release 5.0.0" banner while actual current npm is 6.0.0 (shipped 2026-07-12, Hono API cutover) — 10 releases and 12 days behind. releases.mdx explicitly promises the two pages "change together or not at all"; that promise is currently broken on both sides, since releases.mdx's own matrix also stops at 5.10.1 and is missing the 6.0.0 row. Whatever else the cleanup plan does, re-verifying and re-dating these two pages together should be near the top of the list — it's the biggest gap between what the docs claim and what's true.
- **Duplication cluster: licensing/index.md vs commercial.md/open-source.md.** The overview page repeats each detail page's opening paragraph almost word-for-word. Recommend trimming index.md to short summaries + links rather than merging the detail pages away — the detail pages are the correct place for the full terms.
- **open-source.md has a real content bug, not just staleness**: it describes Mailwoman as maintained by "a team" that takes donations, directly contradicting licensing/index.md's "individual developer" framing (which matches AGENTS.md's stated ownership). Worth a one-line fix independent of the broader dedup rewrite.
- **The api.mdx / getting-started.mdx entry-point contradiction is real and confirmed by reading both pages.** api.mdx states in its very first subsection that `createRuntimePipeline` is "the recommended entry point." getting-started.mdx's first code sample instead instantiates `NeuralAddressClassifier` directly and calls `.parse()`; `createRuntimePipeline` only shows up two sections later, framed as an upgrade ("using the staged pipeline") rather than the default path. This is exactly the fork-in-the-road problem the cleanup plan already flags for getting-started.mdx's opening.
- **sotm-2026-talk-proposal.mdx's stale claim is worth a one-line fix even though the page stays a deliberate nav orphan**: it says the source repo was "scheduled for public release at the end of June 2026," but contributing.md (updated 2026-07-11, three weeks later) still describes the repo as "in the final stage of preparation for public release." The proposal is making a promise the rest of the docs say hasn't been kept yet.
- **Recipes are the healthiest cluster in this scope** — all 8 pages are current, well-written, honest about tradeoffs (real benchmark numbers in parallel-csv-ingest.md, real cost math in multi-service-geocoding.md), and need no action beyond normal maintenance. The only nit is timezones.md's frontmatter `id` (`timezone-lookup`) not matching its filename (`timezones.md`), which is a URL-slug trap if anyone later renames the file expecting the URL to follow.
- **contributing.md has zero measured inbound links** despite being the designated front door for bug reports and contributions — worth deliberately linking from getting-started.mdx or a future "get involved" surface rather than leaving it sidebar-only.
- **reviews/ is correctly excluded as a whole.** All 15 pages are dated, internal, AI-assisted project-direction artifacts (Claude/Codex/DeepSeek reviews, curatorial synthesis, retrospectives) or an agent-directed implementation brief — maintainer/contributor audience throughout, none written for the product-reader/integrator entry paths this reorg is optimizing for. The one exception worth a second look is **2026-06-11-punctuation-survey.md**, which reads as an objective external research survey (how libpostal/Pelias/Nominatim/Photon handle punctuation) rather than a review of Mailwoman itself — it has standalone reference value and is the one candidate in this directory worth promoting to a public Archive or concept page instead of staying excluded. Several of the direction-review pages (the two 2026-05-25 curatorial reviews, in particular) are also direct historical precedents for the very Phase-0 cleanup this inventory is part of — worth a read for whoever designs the final nav, even though they shouldn't ship as public pages themselves.
